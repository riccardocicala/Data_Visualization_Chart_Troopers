Main plot

The plot displays two variables the heating degree days (HDD) index and the cooling degree days (CDD) index and are calculated on a daily basis with the following formulas HDD = max(18-T, 0) and CDD = max(T-21, 0) where T is the temperature of the day.
The HDD is a measure that gives a view of how many degrees of difference there is between the temperature in a house and the temperature outside, thus giving a measure of how much you need to heat; in contrast, the CDD gives a view of how much you need to cool a house relative to the temperature outside.
The plot shows the data with an annual granularity, and the value for a single year is calculated by summing all the values for that specific year.
The data depicted represent European HDDs (in blue) and European CDDs (in red) from 1979 to 2023, obviously being very different values there is a double vertical axis one for HDDs (left) and the other for CDDs (right), on the horizontal axis instead we find the years.

As we can see from the plot, HDDs are steadily decreasing while CDDs are steadily increasing, which indicates to us how there are more and more warmer winters and summers in Europe over the years.
The trend is remarkably marked and this shows us how going forward over the years this situation will be more and more unbearable and will cause greater and greater damage, so something needs to be done to change the trend.

HDD Europe map

The HDD (heating degree days) index is a measure that gives a view of how many degrees of difference there is between the temperature in a house and the temperature outside, thus giving a measure of how much you need to heat and is calculated as HDD = max(18-T, 0) where T is the mean daily temperature.
The data represented have an annual granularity and are calculated as the sum of daily HDDs.
Shown on the map, in gray are the countries that are not in the European union and for which we do not have data, and in a scale of blue the European countries.
Darker shades of blue indicate a higher HDD value and lighter ones the opposite.
On the left is a slider so that the reference year can be chosen.
We can see from the graph the transition of HDDs over the years, noting especially how in the past the shades of blue were much darker throughout Europe, indicating that winters are getting warmer throughout Europe.

Ocean acidification

The heatmap shows the pH value of the ocean as the years change (starting from 1985).
On the horizontal axis we can find the years while on the vertical axis we have a single value, pH.
Shades of red indicate pH values lower than the optimal value 8.2 (in white), the darker red the further away we are from the optimal value.
As we can see going forward in years the sea is becoming more and more acidic and even in 1985 we were already far away from the optimal value that was present before the industrial revolution.
Ocean acidification can create conditions that eat away at the minerals used by oysters, clams, lobsters, shrimp, coral reefs, and other marine life to build their shells and skeletons.
In addition, too rapid a change in pH could lead to other life forms becoming extinct, unable to adapt so quickly to these changes.

European economic losses

So it is also important to show the fact that we are the first to lose from this change, in fact the plot shows the rolling average of the last 30 years of european econimical losses due to climate change as the years change.
This measurement is perfect for showing the trend of a variable, which as in this case points out to us how economic losses are steadily and steeply increasing.
On the horizontal axis, in fact, we have the years, while on the vertical axis we have the economic losses in millions of euros, and we can see how only between 2011 and 2012 and between 2019 and 2020 this trend has dropped slightly while in all other years it is always increasing.

EU Progress on Renewable Energy Share

This plot represents the share of renewable energy used from all the countries, expressed as a percentage.
This indicator tracks progress towards the EU's renewable energy targets.

The data shows that the European Union average has been steadily increasing from 2004 to 2023, rising from 10% to 25%.

Some countries, such as Sweden, Finland, and Denmark, significantly exceed this average with strong growth.

Others, like Germany, Italy, France, and Spain, remain close to the EU average with moderate increases.

Countries such as Poland and Luxembourg are often below the average but show improving trends in recent years.

Renewable Energy Production by Country: Trends and Unique Strategies

This plot represents the top five countries in the European Union ordere by total energy production capacity, and the European Union total sum.
It indicates the percentage of energy production by type, including Hydro, Geothermal, Wind, Solar and Biofuels for each country.

Sweden uses less solar energy compared to other countries, likely due to its long, dark winters and limited sunlight hours, making solar less efficient. However, it compensates by utilizing more biofuels, likely due to the availability of biomass resources and a focus on sustainable energy sources.
Italy is the only country in this group with a notable use of geothermal energy, which is driven by its geological characteristics.
Germany, on the other hand, uses less hydroelectric energy, likely due to limited suitable sites for large-scale hydro power generation.

In general, all countries rely heavily on wind, solar, and hydro energy for their renewable production.

Fresh Nuclear Fuel Production in the EU

The histogram represents the production of fresh fuel elements, expressed in tonnes of Heavy Metal (tHM), across all the countries in the European Union that haven't banned yet nuclear energy.
The production of fresh fuel elements refers to the creation and fabrication of new fuel components (usually uranium or plutonium-based) that are used in nuclear reactors.
These fuel elements are essential for sustaining the nuclear fission process that generates energy within the reactor.

France is the leading producer with 570 tHM, indicating a strong nuclear energy sector and possibly a significant number of reactors in operation.
Sweden, with 360 tHM, also has a relatively large nuclear energy industry, reflecting its reliance on nuclear power for energy production.
Spain and Romania follow with 280 tHM and 210 tHM, respectively, suggesting smaller but notable contributions to the production of fresh nuclear fuel.
Germany, with 180 tHM, has the lowest production among these countries, which could be due to its ongoing transition away from nuclear energy in favor of renewable sources following the decision to phase out nuclear plants in the coming years.

Environmental Taxes Across EU Countries

This map shows the Environmental Taxes paid by industries, households and non-residents for all EU Member State, in millions of euro.
Darker colours imply higher taxes, while lighter colours mean lower taxes.

Large economies like Germany, France, Italy, and Spain collect the most environmental taxes due to their size and policies, while smaller countries like Malta and Cyprus contribute less.
Nordic countries like Sweden and Finland collect significant amounts relative to their size, reflecting advanced policies.
Emerging economies like Romania and Bulgaria have room for growth.

Investments in Climate Change Mitigation

This graph represents the relationship between Environmental Taxes (vertical axis), GDP (horizontal axis) and Investments in Climate Change Mitigation.

  • Vertical Axis (Environmental Taxes): Shows the amount of environmental taxes paid by each country (in millions of euro).
  • Horizontal Axis (GDP): Represents the GDP per capita of each country relative to the European Union's average GDP per capita (100 being the average; e.g., 120 means a GDP 20% higher than the average).
  • Dashed verticalRed line: Represents the average GDP.

  • Bubble Size: Indicates the amount of investment each country makes; larger bubbles mean higher investments.
  • Bubble Color: Reflects the same metric as the size:
    • Red: Low investment.
    • Blue: Moderate investment.
    • Green: High investment.

Investments in Climate Change Mitigation

As expected countries with higher environmental taxes, such as Germany, Italy and France, invest more money in climate change mitigation.
It is interesting to note that, for countries with the same level of taxes paid, those with a higher GDP tend to invest more; with the exceptions of Ireland and Luxembourg.
But this could be due to the fact that they are very small, especially Luxembourg, and therefore lack the physical space to make larger investments, such as building dams, solar installations, or wind turbines.

It is also curious to observe that almost every country collects significantly more in environmental taxes than they actually spend on climate change mitigation...
